📖 Chapter Overview
🔬 Core Concepts

A mixture is formed when two or more substances are combined without any chemical reaction. Unlike compounds, mixtures retain the individual properties of their components and can be separated by physical methods.

Homogeneous mixtures (solutions) have uniform composition throughout — salt dissolved in water looks and tastes the same in every drop. Heterogeneous mixtures like sand-in-water or oil-in-water have visible, non-uniform distribution of components.

The concentration of a solution tells us how much solute is dissolved. It can be expressed as % mass-by-mass (m/m), % mass-by-volume (m/v), or % volume-by-volume (v/v) depending on the nature of the components.

A solution that holds the maximum possible solute at a given temperature is saturated. Beyond that, no more dissolves — any excess remains as a solid at the bottom.

⚗️ Methods of Separation
  • 🫙
    Filtration
    Separates insoluble solids from liquids using porous filter paper.
  • 💧
    Evaporation
    Removes solvent by heating; recovers dissolved solid.
  • 🌡️
    Distillation
    Separates miscible liquids with ≥25°C boiling-point difference.
  • ❄️
    Crystallisation
    Produces pure crystals from saturated solution by slow cooling.
  • 🌀
    Centrifugation
    Rapid spinning separates colloidal / fine particles from liquid.
  • 🌫️
    Sublimation
    Separates subliming solids (camphor, iodine) from non-subliming ones.
  • 🎨
    Chromatography
    Separates dyes/pigments based on differential movement on paper.
✨ Key Takeaways
  • Mixture vs Compound: Mixtures have variable composition and are separated by physical methods; compounds have fixed composition and need chemical methods.
  • Solution Components: Solute (dissolved substance) + Solvent (dissolving medium) = Solution. SI unit of concentration is mol/L, but school level uses % expressions.
  • % m/m Formula: (Mass of solute ÷ Mass of solution) × 100. Denominator is total solution, NOT solvent alone.
  • Saturated Solution: Cannot dissolve more solute at a given temperature. Cooling a saturated solution forces excess solute to crystallise out.
  • Tyndall Effect: Colloids scatter light (beam visible); true solutions do NOT (particles <1 nm too small to scatter).
  • Colloid vs Suspension: Colloid: 1–1000 nm, does NOT settle. Suspension: >1000 nm, DOES settle on standing.
  • Distillation: Simple distillation needs ≥25°C boiling-point difference. Fractional distillation (fractionating column) needed for <25°C difference.
  • Coagulation: Adding alum (fitkari) makes fine suspended particles clump → flocs settle easily. Used in water purification.
  • Solubility & Temperature: For most solids, solubility increases with temperature. For gases, solubility DECREASES with temperature.
🧠 Where You See This in Real Life
🍵
Filtering Tea
A tea strainer filters out insoluble tea leaves from the liquid — classic filtration in your kitchen.
🏖️
Sand & Water
Sand doesn't dissolve — it forms a suspension. Filtration easily separates sand from water.
🫒
Oil & Water
Oil and water are immiscible — they form layers. A separating funnel drains the lower (denser) liquid.
🌊
Sea Salt Production
Seawater is spread in shallow pans. Sunlight evaporates water, leaving pure salt crystals behind.
🧪
Alum in Water
Alum is added to muddy water in villages — it coagulates fine particles into flocs that settle.
🩸
Blood Testing
Blood is centrifuged to separate plasma, RBCs, and platelets — centrifugation of a colloid.
⛽
Petroleum Refining
Crude oil is fractionally distilled to produce petrol, diesel, kerosene — boiling points differ by <25°C.
🌫️
Foggy Headlights
Fog scatters car headlight beams (Tyndall effect) — fog is a liquid-in-gas colloid.
🍬
Mishri (Rock Sugar)
Sugar crystals grow large and beautiful when a saturated solution is cooled slowly — crystallisation at home.
⚠️ Common Mistakes Students Make
Mistake #1
% m/m denominator error
Students use mass of SOLVENT in denominator instead of mass of SOLUTION (solute + solvent). Always use total solution mass.
Mistake #2
Mixture vs Compound confusion
Mixtures have variable composition and NO energy change on forming. Compounds have fixed ratios and ARE formed by chemical reactions.
Mistake #3
Colloid vs Suspension
Colloid particles (1–1000 nm) do NOT settle. Suspension particles (>1000 nm) DO settle. Milk = colloid. Chalk-water = suspension.
Mistake #4
Simple vs Fractional Distillation
Simple distillation needs ≥25°C boiling-point difference. For smaller gaps (like crude oil fractions), fractional distillation is needed.
Mistake #5
Tyndall Effect in Solutions
True solutions do NOT show Tyndall effect — particles are <1 nm, too small to scatter light. Only colloids and suspensions scatter light.
Mistake #6
Gas solubility and temperature
Most students think solubility always increases with temperature. For GASES it is opposite — solubility DECREASES as temperature rises.
