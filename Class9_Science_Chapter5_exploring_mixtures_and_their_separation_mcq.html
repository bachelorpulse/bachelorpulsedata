📖 Understanding the Chapter

A mixture is formed when two or more substances combine without any chemical reaction. Unlike compounds, mixtures retain the individual properties of their components and can always be separated by physical methods. Mixtures are everywhere — the air we breathe, the salt water in oceans, the blood flowing in our veins.

Mixtures are broadly divided into two types. In a homogeneous mixture (solution), composition is uniform throughout — every drop of salt water tastes equally salty. In a heterogeneous mixture, components are visibly non-uniform — sand in water, oil on water. The key technique for telling them apart is observing whether the components are distinguishable by the naked eye or a microscope.

The concentration of a solution tells us how much solute is dissolved. It is expressed as % m/m (mass of solute ÷ mass of solution × 100), % m/v, or % v/v depending on the state of the components. A solution that has dissolved the maximum possible amount of solute at a given temperature is called saturated — beyond this point, excess solute simply remains undissolved at the bottom.

Not all mixtures behave like simple solutions. Colloids (particle size 1–1000 nm) appear uniform but are actually heterogeneous — they scatter light (Tyndall effect) and do not settle on standing. Suspensions (particle size >1000 nm) are clearly heterogeneous, visibly cloudy, and do settle. Choosing the right separation method depends entirely on understanding which type of mixture you are dealing with.

✨ Key Takeaways
  • Mixture vs Compound: Mixtures have variable composition and are separated by physical methods; compounds have fixed composition and need chemical methods.
  • % m/m Formula: (Mass of solute ÷ Mass of solution) × 100. Denominator is TOTAL solution mass — not just the solvent.
  • Saturated Solution: Cannot dissolve more solute at a given temperature. Cooling forces excess solute to crystallise out.
  • Tyndall Effect: Colloids scatter light (beam visible); true solutions do NOT — particles <1 nm are too small to scatter.
  • Colloid vs Suspension: Colloid: 1–1000 nm, does NOT settle. Suspension: >1000 nm, DOES settle on standing.
  • Distillation: Simple distillation needs ≥25°C boiling-point difference; fractional distillation needed for <25°C difference (e.g. crude oil).
  • Coagulation: Alum (fitkari) makes fine suspended particles clump into flocs that settle — used in water purification.
  • Gas Solubility: For most solids, solubility increases with temperature. For gases, solubility DECREASES with temperature.
  • Crystallisation: Slow cooling of a saturated solution gives large, pure crystals. Rapid cooling gives small, impure crystals.
